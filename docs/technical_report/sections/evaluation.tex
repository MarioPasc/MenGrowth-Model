\subsection{Variance Decomposition Framework}
\label{sec:eval:variance}

The central analytical contribution of this thesis is a variance
decomposition quantifying the marginal predictive value of each complexity
stage. We define a nested model hierarchy:

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Nested model hierarchy for variance decomposition.}
  \label{tab:model_hierarchy}
  \begin{tabular}{clcp{5.5cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Model} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Params} &
      \textbf{Inputs} \\
    \midrule
    $M_0$ & Population mean only & 2 & $t$ \\
    $M_1$ & ScalarGP (Stage~1) & 5 & $t$ \\
    $M_2$ & LME (Stage~1) & 6 & $t$, patient ID \\
    $M_3$ & HGP (Stage~1) & 5 & $t$, patient ID, LME mean \\
    $M_4$ & Severity model (Stage~2) & $\sim$3 + $N$ & $t$, $s_i$ \\
    $M_5$ & Deep features (Stage~3) & $\sim$9 & $t$, $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{Marginal $\Delta R^2$}

For each successive model, the marginal contribution is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta R^2_k = R^2(M_k) - R^2(M_{k-1}),
  \label{eq:delta_r2}
\end{equation}
where $R^2$ is computed from LOPO-CV predictions (out-of-sample). The
$\Delta R^2$ values are not necessarily positive: a more complex model
may perform worse out-of-sample due to overfitting. A negative $\Delta R^2$
is strong evidence that the additional complexity is not warranted at the
current sample size.

\subsubsection{Statistical Significance}

To determine whether a $\Delta R^2$ is statistically significant, we use
two complementary tests:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=*,labelwidth=!]
  \item[Paired permutation test.] For each LOPO-CV fold $i$, compute
    the per-fold squared error for models $M_k$ and $M_{k-1}$. Under the
    null hypothesis $H_0: \Delta R^2 = 0$, the fold-level error differences
    are exchangeable. We permute the signs of $\{\text{SE}_i^{(k)} -
    \text{SE}_i^{(k-1)}\}_{i=1}^N$ over $B = 10{,}000$ iterations and
    compute the $p$-value as the fraction of permutations with
    $|\Delta \overline{\text{SE}}| \geq |\Delta \overline{\text{SE}}_{\text{obs}}|$.

  \item[BCa bootstrap CIs.] Bias-corrected and accelerated (BCa) bootstrap
    confidence intervals \cite{efron1987bca} for $R^2$ and $\Delta R^2$,
    using $B = 2{,}000$ resamples of the LOPO-CV fold results. The 95\%
    CI for $\Delta R^2$ that excludes zero provides an additional
    significance criterion.
\end{description}

\subsubsection{Decision Rule}

The complexity ladder advances to the next stage only if:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $\Delta R^2 > 0$ (positive marginal contribution).
  \item The paired permutation test yields $p < 0.05$.
  \item The 95\% BCa bootstrap CI for $\Delta R^2$ excludes zero.
\end{enumerate}
If any condition fails, the simpler model is preferred and the higher stage
is reported as a negative result.

  \begin{tabular}{clcp{5.5cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{ID} & \textbf{Variable} & \textbf{Conditions} &
      \textbf{Primary metric} \\
    \midrule
    A1 & Segmentation model & \{frozen BSF, LoRA-adapted, fine-tuned\}
      & Stage~1 Vol $R^2$ (LOPO) \\
    A2 & GP mean function & \{zero, linear, Gompertz\}
      & Stage~1 Vol $R^2$ (LOPO) \\
    A3 & Growth function (Stage~2) & \{sigmoid, reduced Gompertz, CMNN\}
      & Stage~2 $R^2$ (LOPO) \\
    A4 & Severity estimation inputs & \{clinical, clinical+deep, deep-only\}
      & Severity--growth correlation \\
    A5 & LoRA rank (Stage~3) & $\{2, 4, 8, 16, 32\}$
      & Dice, probe $R^2$ \\
    A6 & PCA components (Stage~3) & $\{3, 4, 5, 6, 8\}$
      & Stage~3 $R^2$ (LOPO) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{A1: Segmentation Model Comparison}

Stage~1 requires accurate tumour segmentation to extract volume. This
ablation compares three segmentation sources:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Frozen BSF:} Direct application of the pretrained
    BrainSegFounder checkpoint without any meningioma-specific adaptation.
  \item \textbf{LoRA-adapted BSF:} After Phase~1 LoRA training on
    BraTS-MEN~2024 (\cref{sec:lora}).
  \item \textbf{Fine-tuned BSF:} Full fine-tuning of the decoder (encoder
    frozen), as a more expensive but potentially more accurate alternative.
\end{itemize}
The segmentation quality directly affects Stage~1 volume accuracy and
therefore all downstream predictions. This ablation determines whether LoRA
adaptation is necessary for growth prediction (as opposed to segmentation
quality \textit{per se}).

\subsubsection{A2: Gompertz versus Linear Mean}

Motivated by the growth biology literature (\cref{sec:bg:growth_biology}),
this ablation tests whether a Gompertz mean function
(\cref{eq:gompertz_mean}) improves the HGP over a linear mean. The Gompertz
mean adds one parameter ($\beta$, the deceleration constant) and encodes the
prior that meningioma growth decelerates---a strong biological prior that may
be especially valuable at small $N$.

\subsubsection{A6: PCA Components for Stage~3}

The number of PCA components $d_{\text{PCA}}$ controls the
complexity--information tradeoff in Stage~3. Too few components discard
growth-relevant information; too many introduce noise dimensions that the
GP cannot reliably fit at $N = 33$.

\subsection{R1 Methodology Revision}
\label{sec:eval:revision}

Empirical findings from the initial pipeline (before the complexity ladder
reframing) motivated three principled revisions.

\subsubsection{Shape Partition Failure}

Across all experimental conditions, the shape partition achieved
$R^2 \leq 0.11$ for linear probes and approximately zero for RBF probes.
Cross-domain shape transfer was uniformly catastrophic ($R^2 = -2.00$).
This failure is biologically grounded: gliomas are infiltrative and
irregular, while meningiomas are well-circumscribed and dural-based.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Shape probe $R^2$ across experimental conditions.}
  \label{tab:shape_failure}
  \begin{tabular}{lccl}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Condition} & \textbf{Linear $R^2$} & \textbf{RBF $R^2$} &
      \textbf{Cross-domain} \\
    \midrule
    Frozen baseline   & 0.08  & $\approx 0$ & $-2.00$ \\
    MEN LoRA $r$=8    & 0.11  & $\approx 0$ & $-2.00$ \\
    Dual LoRA $r$=8   & $-0.06$ & $\approx 0.15$ & $-2.00$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Decision:} Drop the shape partition from the SDP (Stage~3).

\subsubsection{Location: Temporally Static}

Meningiomas are extra-axial, dural-attached tumours that do not migrate.
Modelling location as a time-varying latent dimension is statistically
vacuous. Within-domain location $R^2$ is reasonable (0.35~MEN, 0.68~GLI),
confirming the information is present in the features but does not need
temporal modelling.

\textbf{Decision:} Relocate location from temporal latent dimension to
static GP covariate.

\subsubsection{Volume Target Simplification}

The 4-dimensional volume target $[\log V_{\text{total}}, \log V_{\text{NCR}},
\log V_{\text{ED}}, \log V_{\text{ET}}]$ is problematic because sub-compartment
segmentation quality is poor (TC Dice 0.40 on MEN) and whole-tumour volume
is the only clinically relevant growth endpoint for meningiomas.

\textbf{Decision:} Reduce to a single target
$v^* = \log(V_{\text{WT}} + 1) \in \R^1$.

\subsubsection{The Complexity Ladder as a Methodological Response}

The R1 revision also motivated the fundamental reframing from a linear
pipeline (LoRA $\to$ SDP $\to$ GP on latent trajectories) to the three-stage
complexity ladder. The key insight was that the marginal benefit of LoRA
adaptation for downstream feature quality was $\Delta R^2 = +0.016$
(\cref{sec:eval:marginality}), raising the question of whether the entire
representation learning pipeline was necessary. The complexity ladder
addresses this question directly by placing simple volumetric models first
and requiring each additional stage to demonstrate measurable improvement.

% 9.7 LoRA Marginality Discussion
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{LoRA Marginality}
\label{sec:eval:marginality}

A notable finding from the initial pipeline is the marginal benefit of LoRA
adaptation for downstream feature quality. The frozen BrainSegFounder
baseline achieves $R^2 = 0.794$ for volume probing, while the best LoRA
condition adds only $\Delta R^2 = +0.016$ (within noise). This suggests that
BrainSegFounder's SSL pretraining on 41,400+ subjects already produces
features with strong meningioma volume decodability.

Several hypotheses explain this observation:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{SSL pretraining captures domain-general features.} The UK
    Biobank pretraining stage exposes the encoder to massive anatomical
    diversity, producing features that generalise across brain pathologies.
  \item \textbf{LoRA's low-rank constraint limits adaptation.} With
    $r = 8$ and $<0.32\%$ trainable parameters, LoRA may not have
    sufficient capacity to substantially restructure the 768-dimensional
    feature space.
  \item \textbf{Segmentation is a weak proxy for semantic quality.}
    Improving segmentation Dice does not necessarily improve the linear
    decodability of aggregate semantic properties.
\end{enumerate}

This finding motivates the complexity ladder design: if LoRA provides
marginal benefit for feature quality, the entire Stage~3 representation
learning pipeline may provide marginal benefit for growth prediction.
The variance decomposition will quantify this directly.

\subsection{Stage~2 Gate Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:eval:stage2_failure}

The severity model (Stage~2) achieves $R^2 = -3.54$ under LOPO-CV,
substantially worse than all Stage~1 models and even worse than the population
mean ($M_0$). This result decisively fails the Stage~2 gate criterion
($R^2_{\text{Stage~2}} \geq R^2_{\text{Stage~1}}$), preventing advancement to
Stage~3.

\subsubsection{Root Cause: Ordinal Time Representation}

The primary cause is the \textbf{ordinal time representation} available in the
longitudinal cohort: scan indices (1, 2, 3, \ldots) rather than calendar dates.
The severity model's quantile transform maps absolute elapsed times to quantile
space, but when times are ordinal, the transform becomes a near-identity mapping
that destroys the temporal magnitude information needed to differentiate growth
rates. A patient with two scans 6~months apart and a patient with two scans
5~years apart both receive ordinal times $\{0, 1\}$, making their growth
trajectories indistinguishable in quantile space.

\subsubsection{Secondary Factors}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Reduced Gompertz over-parameterisation.} With $N=30$ patients
    and ordinal time, the 3~population parameters of the reduced Gompertz
    growth function cannot be reliably estimated.
  \item \textbf{Regression head degeneration.} The severity regression head
    predicts $s_i$ from baseline features, but when the jointly-estimated
    severities $\{s_i\}$ are poorly identified (due to ordinal time), the
    regression targets contain noise rather than signal.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Implications}

This failure is a \emph{finding}, not a deficiency of the framework. It
empirically demonstrates that the severity model requires exact timestamps ---
ordinal time is insufficient for a temporal normalisation approach. The
complexity ladder correctly identifies this: the gate mechanism prevents a
failing model from propagating errors to Stage~3.

\textbf{Remediation.} Obtaining exact acquisition dates from the clinical
information system would enable re-evaluation of Stage~2. With physically
meaningful elapsed times, the quantile transform can recover genuine growth
rate information, and the severity model may provide the patient-level
differentiation it is designed to capture.
