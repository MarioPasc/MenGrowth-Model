% --- Abstract ---
\begin{abstract}
Meningiomas are the most prevalent primary intracranial tumours, yet predicting
their growth trajectories from longitudinal MRI remains an open challenge due to
heterogeneous growth dynamics and limited longitudinal data. We address the
question: at what level of model complexity does additional sophistication stop
improving out-of-sample prediction when the longitudinal cohort contains only
$N = 31$--$58$ patients? We organise the investigation as a three-stage
complexity ladder. Stage~1 (volumetric baseline) segments tumours, extracts
whole-tumour volume, and models scalar volume trajectories with GP and LME
models. Stage~2 (latent severity) introduces a single patient-specific severity
parameter $s_i \in [0, 1]$ via a nonlinear mixed-effects model connected to
Item Response Theory and reduced Gompertz dynamics. Stage~3 (representation
learning) applies BrainSegFounder---a SwinUNETR foundation model pretrained on
41,400+ subjects---adapted via Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), followed by a
Supervised Disentangled Projection (SDP) and PCA-compressed GP prediction with
ARD kernels. A cross-cutting variance decomposition quantifies the marginal
$\Delta R^2$ of each stage via Leave-One-Patient-Out Cross-Validation, with
paired permutation tests and BCa bootstrap confidence intervals. Each stage
must earn its place: the complexity ladder advances only when the next stage
demonstrates statistically significant improvement. We report the first
out-of-sample prediction benchmarks for meningioma growth, domain gap analysis
for the glioma-to-meningioma transfer, LoRA ablation results, and discuss
methodology revisions motivated by empirical findings including the failure of
shape disentanglement and the marginal benefit of LoRA adaptation ($\Delta R^2
= +0.016$) for downstream feature quality.
\end{abstract}
